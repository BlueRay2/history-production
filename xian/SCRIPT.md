# SCRIPT - Xi'an

**Working title:** *Xi'an - The City That Was the Centre of History Three Times*  
**Target length:** 7-10 min (~1,050-1,150 words narration at 135-150 wpm)  
**Narrator:** Documentary register, spacious BBC cadence, with selective ElevenLabs v3 tags.  

CTAs are welded inline and marked ⟨CTA-1⟩ through ⟨CTA-4⟩. Open loops are planted and paid off with ⟨OL-1⟩ through ⟨OL-4⟩.

## Act I - The Buried Emperor

### Scene 1 - Cold Open
**Time:** 0:00-0:15 | **Narration:** locked hook

> [cinematic tone] In nineteen seventy-four, a farmer digging a well in northwest China struck something hard. He'd just found the private army of China's first emperor - eight thousand life-size clay soldiers, each with a different face, entombed for two thousand two hundred years. Two kilometres away lies his mausoleum, still sealed, because the soil there runs with mercury. ⟨OL-1⟩ This is the story of Xi'an - the city where the First Emperor still keeps his court. And that's only the first of three global turning points that happened here.

**Visual motif:** Black screen to shovel strike, then a terracotta face emerging from wet earth, then a fast push toward the sealed mound beyond the pits.

### Scene 2 - The Farmer and the Army
**Time:** 0:15-1:10 | **Narration:** ~130 words

> [storytelling tone] The farmer was Yang Zhifa, working in Lintong during a dry spring in nineteen seventy-four, digging for water where no one expected an empire to be waiting. What he and his neighbours opened was not a tomb chamber but the edge of an entire military theatre: trenches filled with infantry, archers, cavalry, chariots, officers. Today the estimate sits at roughly eight thousand figures.
>
> Each face feels individual because it was meant to feel individual. Not portrait sculpture in the modern sense, but a disciplined production system that still left room for expression: a heavier jaw here, a different moustache there, a veteran's calm in the eyes of fired clay.
>
> ⟨CTA-1⟩ Before we go further - if you want to know how one city is the accidental centre of three global stories, the tomb, the Silk Road, and the war that made the modern world, subscribe. I'll spend the next seven minutes telling you why Xi'an is the single city on earth that's been the centre of history three separate times.

**Visual motif:** Sepia recreation of Yang Zhifa at the well, cross-cut with modern drone shots over Pit One and slow close-ups of different warrior faces.

### Scene 3 - What Qin Buried
**Time:** 1:10-3:00 | **Narration:** ~230 words

> Qin Shi Huang died in two hundred and ten BCE, but the project had begun long before that. Sima Qian, in the *Shiji*, says seven hundred thousand workers were drawn into building the mausoleum complex over decades. The army we see now was only one part of that machine of afterlife rule.
>
> And it did not look like this. [pause] The warriors were originally painted in lacquer and mineral pigments - reds, blues, greens, pinks. When modern excavations exposed them to air, many of those colours oxidised and curled away within minutes. The famous brown ranks of Xi'an are, in part, the colour of loss.
>
> Their bronze weapons created another myth. If films like *The Mummy* left you with the idea that Qin craftsmen had discovered chrome-plating, they had not. A twenty nineteen paper in *Scientific Reports* showed that the chromium traces were contamination from lacquer on grips and shafts. The real preservation came from bronze alloy and soil chemistry, not lost technology.
>
> But the greater fact waits beyond the pits. ⟨OL-1⟩ Two kilometres away, under the great mound, lies the emperor's mausoleum itself. Modern surveys have found mercury in the surrounding soil at around one hundred times background levels - a striking confirmation of Sima Qian's account of rivers of mercury flowing through a model empire below ground. And the tomb remains unexcavated because modern archaeology knows how easily one opening can destroy what two millennia kept intact.

**Visual motif:** Candlelit reconstruction of artisans painting a warrior's face, hard cut to pigment flaking in museum footage, then microscope and bronze blade shots before a slow aerial climb over the unopened burial mound.

## Act II - The Open City

### Scene 4 - A Different Capital on the Same Valley
**Time:** 3:00-4:20 | **Narration:** ~180 words

> [storytelling tone] Eighty years after Qin died, a different city rose on the same valley. ⟨OL-2⟩ Its name was Chang'an - Han first, Tang later - and from here the Silk Road ran westward into Central Asia, Persia, and the Mediterranean world.
>
> By the Tang, this was not merely an imperial capital. It was a grid-plan metropolis on a scale medieval Europe could barely imagine. The avenues were regimented, wards were walled, and at the northern edge sat the Daming Palace - later ruled from by Wu Zetian, the only woman to rule China as emperor - a complex about four and a half times the size of Beijing's Forbidden City.
>
> ⟨OL-2⟩ By around seven hundred and fifty CE, Chang'an had roughly one million people. Contemporary London had about ten thousand. [pause]
>
> ⟨CTA-2⟩ Quick question - in seven hundred and fifty CE, Chang'an was the world's largest city, with a million people. Meanwhile London had about ten thousand. [pause] Drop the city you're watching from in the comments - I'm curious how many people are watching from a place that existed at all in the Tang era.

**Visual motif:** CGI map rises from the Qin mound into the Han-Tang street grid, then glides toward Daming Palace before freezing on a side-by-side population comparison card that holds for several seconds.

### Scene 5 - The Western Market
**Time:** 4:20-6:00 | **Narration:** ~230 words

> The Western Market was where that scale became human. Sogdians running taverns. Persians and Arabs trading glass, silver, and horses. Turks, Indians, Japanese, Koreans, diplomats, monks, translators, officials. Historian Susan Whitfield describes Silk Road cities as places where distance became ordinary. Chang'an may be the clearest example.
>
> ⟨OL-3⟩ By seven hundred and fifty CE, this single city held five religions in working worship. Buddhism was everywhere. Taoism stood close to the state. Zoroastrian communities kept their fire rites. Islam had already arrived with merchants from the west. And in seven hundred and forty-two CE, Xi'an's Great Mosque was founded in a form that still says everything about the city: from the street it looks like a Chinese temple complex, but its prayer axis turns toward Mecca.
>
> Christianity arrived too, far earlier than most viewers expect. ⟨OL-3⟩ The proof still stands in stone: the Nestorian Stele, erected in seven hundred and eighty-one CE, carved in Chinese and Syriac, recording how the monk Alopen reached Chang'an in six hundred and thirty-five CE and was received at the Tang court. So when people imagine medieval civilization as a story told only from Europe outward, Xi'an quietly breaks the frame.

**Visual motif:** Lantern-lit market reconstruction with mixed Eurasian faces and goods, then a serene track through the Great Mosque courtyards before ending on the bilingual Nestorian Stele in cool museum light.

## Act III - The Kidnapping

### Scene 6 - The City After Empire
**Time:** 6:00-6:50 | **Narration:** ~120 words

> Capitals moved. Dynasties fell. In the Ming, the city took the name Xi'an - Western Peace - and new walls rose over older foundations, turning imperial memory into brick and battlement. [pause]
>
> Then, a thousand years after Tang Chang'an, history bent here again. ⟨OL-4⟩ In December nineteen thirty-six, Chiang Kai-shek came to Xi'an to force China's divided armies to keep fighting the Communists while Japan was already pressing deeper into North China.
>
> He stayed at Huaqing Pool, beneath hills once built for emperors' pleasure. ⟨CTA-3⟩ If you've made it this far - and especially if you did not know the Xi'an Incident even happened - like this and stay with me. What comes next is the thing most Western history classes skip.

**Visual motif:** Bicycle riders on the Ming wall dissolve into winter mist over Huaqing Pool, then a quiet bedroom recreation before the first distant crack of rifle fire.

### Scene 7 - The Morning That Forced an Alliance
**Time:** 6:50-8:30 | **Narration:** ~200 words

> Before dawn on December twelfth, mutinous troops under Zhang Xueliang and Yang Hucheng stormed the resort. Chiang fled in his nightclothes into the cold hills above Huaqing Pool, stumbled, hid, and was captured by his own side. It sounds almost absurd in miniature. In consequence, it was enormous.
>
> ⟨OL-4⟩ The Xi'an Incident forced negotiations that produced the Second United Front - a temporary alliance between Nationalists and Communists against imperial Japan. The Chinese civil war did not end, but it paused. And that pause mattered. One city that had once guarded an emperor's afterlife, and then opened China to the world, now became the place where the pressure of invasion overruled ideology.
>
> [cinematic tone] That is Xi'an's pattern. A buried court. An open city. A kidnapping that changed the course of the war in Asia.
>
> Today high-speed rail lines run past the old walls. In Lintong, the soldiers still stand. In the Muslim Quarter, the legacy of Tang exchange still cooks and prays and bargains in the street. Xi'an does not feel like a ruin. It feels like a city still living with all its previous names.
>
> ⟨CTA-4⟩ Which of the three turning points surprised you the most - the eight thousand soldiers, the million-person cosmopolitan capital, or the kidnapping that pushed the world toward war? Tell me in the comments. Next video, we leave Asia - and visit a city that forgot it was once an empire.

**Visual motif:** Pre-dawn chase in the hills above Huaqing Pool gives way to archival stills and newspaper headlines, then resolves into present-day Xi'an street life, the city walls at dusk, and one last held close-up on a terracotta warrior's face.
