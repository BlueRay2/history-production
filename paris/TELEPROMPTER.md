### Scene 03 — Setup: Why Paris Needed a Tower in 1889 (0:30–1:30)

1889. France is turning one hundred. A century earlier, the Revolution had cut off the head of Louis the Sixteenth, and the country wants to mark the anniversary in style. Paris is hosting the World's Fair — a 19th-century Expo where nations flex their technology, culture, and engineering.

The French government wants a symbol nobody has ever built. They open a contest for a centerpiece — a giant structure right in the heart of Paris, next to the Champ de Mars. One hundred and seven proposals come in. Most are stone towers, monumental arches, neo-Gothic cathedrals. The winner looks insane: a three-hundred-meter iron lattice that resembles the unfinished skeleton of a skyscraper.

The architect: Gustave Eiffel — already famous for bridges, viaducts, and the inner iron framework of the Statue of Liberty, delivered to New York in 1886. The man knows metal and scale. To Parisians of the 1880s, though, a giant iron tower as the symbol of France lands about as well as proposing today to swap Big Ben for a laser pyramid in central London.

### Scene 04 — Construction: Numbers, Speed, Money (1:30–3:10)

Construction begins on January 28, 1887. While it's still going up, the world is taking bets on whether the tower will collapse before the Fair even opens.

The numbers still impress engineers today. Eighteen thousand and thirty-eight iron parts — each forged and machined separately at Eiffel's factory just outside Paris. Two and a half million rivets stitch the structure together. The fit tolerance: tenths of a millimeter. One piece a hair too long, and the section would skew — the tower simply wouldn't come together.

Three hundred workers, every kind of weather, at altitude. And the most unbelievable fact: in over two years of main assembly, only one person dies — and even that wasn't on the structure itself, but later, during elevator installation, when a worker ignored the safety procedure. For the late 19th century — no helmets, no harnesses, no modern lifts — that's a number engineers refused to believe for a long time.

The cost: seven million eight hundred thousand francs. A detail tourist guides usually skip — the government covered only a third. Eiffel put up the rest out of his own pocket.

In exchange, he got a twenty-year right to ticket revenue. Take this in: the eventual symbol of Paris was originally a private investment. Eiffel bet his own capital on it.

March 31, 1889: opening day. Three hundred meters. The tallest structure in the world. That record holds for forty-one years — until the Chrysler Building goes up in New York in 1930.

### Scene 05 — The Manifesto of the 300 (3:10–4:20)

Here's the awkward part. The Parisian elite — writers, painters, composers — went ballistic.

While the tower was still going up, in February 1887, the newspaper Le Temps published an open letter signed by three hundred people — the "Manifesto of the 300." Among the signatories: Charles Gounod, composer of "Faust"; Alexandre Dumas the younger, author of "The Lady of the Camellias"; the poet Sully Prudhomme, soon the first ever winner of the Nobel Prize in Literature; and Charles Garnier, architect of the Paris Opera.

They called the tower — quoting — "dizzyingly absurd," "a giant black factory chimney," and "the shame of Paris."

The most venomous critic was Guy de Maupassant, author of "Bel-Ami." Legend has it he lunched every day at the tower's restaurant. Asked why, he answered:

"It's the only place in Paris where I don't have to look at it."

Parisians nicknamed it the "tragic streetlamp." Papers ran caricatures. The authorities promised: temporary. It was in the contract — in twenty years, in 1909, the tower was to be dismantled and sold for scrap.

---

### Scene 06 — How Radio Saved the Tower (4:20–5:30)

Here comes the part that wasn't in anyone's plan — not Eiffel's, not the government's.

In the early 20th century, humanity starts taking radio seriously. Wireless transmission across tens of kilometers is cutting-edge military tech, and physicists say long-range antennas need a tall mast. The taller, the farther the signal.

In Paris, the highest point is the Eiffel Tower. And Eiffel — fully aware that 1909 is approaching — has been quietly funding radio experiments at its summit since the late 1890s. He's giving his tower a practical function the state can't walk away from.

It works. From 1903 onward, the tower is the main radio station of the French army. During World War One, it intercepts German communications — at the Battle of the Marne in 1914 alone, that interception is credited with saving thousands of lives.

By 1909, when the contract expires, nobody is asking the question anymore. The tower stays. Not as a beauty, not as a symbol — as a strategic military asset.

Eiffel saved his creation through the very science the project was never originally justified by.

### Scene 07 — The Tower Today + 1940 Closure (5:30–6:40)

Today the Eiffel Tower is the most-visited paid monument in the world. Seven million visitors a year. Three hundred and twenty-four meters tall — taller than the original three hundred, thanks to the antennas. Sixty tons of paint every seven years, because raw iron rusts. For the 2024 Olympics, almost the entire structure was restored for around sixty million euros.

One last detail closes the loop on this video's first scene.

June 1940. Hitler walks into occupied Paris and wants to go up the tower. The French elevator operators had cut the cables in advance. When Hitler arrived, the elevators were dead.

The Führer wasn't going to climb three hundred meters of stairs. There's a famous photograph: Hitler standing at the foot of the tower — but never on top of it.

The Eiffel Tower — alone in all of Paris — did not let Hitler in.
