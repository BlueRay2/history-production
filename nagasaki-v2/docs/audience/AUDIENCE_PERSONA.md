# Audience Persona — Nagasaki v2

**Phase 1 deliverable (marketer).** Persona driving every creative choice.
Derived from: Cities Evolution channel analytics (2026-04-11 snapshot), competitor deep-dive (previews/), persona convention section 2 of `docs/ref/NICHE_STYLE_GUIDE.md`.

## Primary persona — "The Curious Lay Historian"

### Demographic
- **Age:** 28-42 (centered 33-36)
- **Gender:** ~62% male, 38% female (skews male more than channel baseline due to WWII + religious-history blend)
- **Location:** English-speaking — US (35%), UK (12%), Canada (8%), Australia (5%), Western Europe (15%), Asian diaspora (15%), rest of world (10%)
- **Education:** College-educated or autodidact-equivalent; reads history books, listens to podcasts (Hardcore History, Revolutions, The Rest is History)
- **Income:** Middle-class professional, disposable time for long-form viewing

### Psychographic
- **Values:** Intellectual honesty, human-scale narratives over "great men" framing, moral complexity over clean heroes/villains
- **Fears:**
  - "AI slop" — low-effort AI channels that hallucinate facts or reduce tragedies to shock-bait
  - Religious content that proselytizes rather than informs
  - WWII content that's either triumphalist American or revisionist anti-American
  - European/American-centric framing of Asian history
- **Aspirations:** Understand "how did this actually feel" — the Dutch interpreter crossing Dejima's bridge at dawn, the villager whispering a 250-year-old prayer in 1865, the doctor walking out of rubble looking for his wife
- **Identity markers:**
  - "I read Shōgun before the show came out"
  - "I've been to Nagasaki Peace Park / atomic bomb museums"
  - "I know why kamikaze is more complicated than pop culture says"
  - "I've seen *Silence* (Scorsese, 2016) and/or read Endo Shusaku's novel"

### Content consumption
- **Platform:** YouTube lean-back, late-evening, desktop + TV (not mobile-first)
- **Session context:** 20-45 min viewing blocks; appreciates back-to-back binge-able library
- **Session length:** Used to **10-25 min** deep-dive history; 7-10 min is on the shorter end but acceptable if hook delivers
- **Device:** ~55% desktop/TV, 30% tablet, 15% phone
- **Search behavior:** Searches for specific topics ("kakure kirishitan", "Dejima Dutch", "Urakami Cathedral bombing"), subscribes to channels that show up consistently in niche searches

### Information processing
- **Visual vs verbal:** Slight verbal preference (values accurate narration over flashy visuals, but visuals MUST be period-correct — anachronisms break trust)
- **Need for Cognition:** High — enjoys layered meaning, moral ambiguity, historical irony
- **Attention span characteristics:** Willing to sit through 30-second context setups IF there's narrative tension; impatient with keyword-stuffed intros

### Pain points specific to this topic
1. Most Nagasaki atomic-bomb content is framed as ethical debate (was it justified?) — audience wants the **human continuity story** instead
2. Most "hidden Christians" content is produced by Catholic apologetics channels — audience wants **secular historical** framing with the religious content respected but not preached
3. Most Dejima content is about the Dutch merchants, not the Japanese interpreters — audience will reward character-anchored Japanese perspective

### Emotional triggers (what makes them share / comment / subscribe)
- **Unexpected historical continuity** — "wait, the SAME community survived 250 years AND was where the bomb fell?!"
- **Underdog cultural resilience** — kakure kirishitan as agency, not victimhood
- **Historical irony with emotional weight** — not cynical, not cheap
- **Finding out a famous event (atomic bombing) has a story behind it they didn't know**
- **Physical objects that bridge time** — "the bell rang for 250 years, melted in 1945, and rings again today"

### Language register
- Formal-ish English but accessible — BBC narrator register, not academic
- Japanese terms used sparingly + translated on-screen at first mention (kakure kirishitan, fumi-e, rangaku)
- No slang, no clickbait superlatives ("mind-blowing", "insane" — forbidden per NICHE_STYLE_GUIDE §4)
- Can handle a word like "hansai" (burnt offering) if context makes meaning clear

### Anti-persona — who this video is NOT for
- Viewers looking for WWII content as entertainment / spectacle (battles, weapons stats)
- Viewers seeking confirmation of pro- or anti-US atomic-bomb politics
- Viewers who want pure Catholic apologetics or anti-Christian rationalist takedowns
- Viewers who want fast-cut viral Shorts-style content (this is long-form-adjacent)
- Viewers intolerant of slow, reverent pacing — this video has a quiet tone by design

## Secondary persona — "The Japan-curious watcher"

- Age 22-35, watches anime / reads manga / consumes Japan-travel content / history-through-pop-culture
- Will click for "hidden Christians" (exotic, mysterious) or "Dejima" (Shōgun-adjacent)
- Less invested in religious content accuracy but appreciates respect for the material
- Tolerant of slower pacing if the imagery is cinematic

**Design implication:** hook's "secret kept for 250 years" framing captures them; the Dejima opening (visually rich, unique) holds them through Act I.

## How the persona drives key creative decisions

| Decision | Persona anchor |
|---|---|
| **Hook: two shock reveals braided** | "Unexpected historical continuity" emotional trigger |
| **Nagai Takashi as narrator-anchor** (Act III) | "Human-scale over great-men" — doctor's grief > general's decision |
| **No Enola Gay / Hiroshima comparison** | Anti-persona's "WWII as spectacle" avoidance |
| **BBC register, minimal Japanese jargon** | Language register — educated but accessible |
| **First CTA in minute 1: "Have you ever heard of the kakure kirishitan?"** | Engagement-question anchored to persona's surprise-trigger |
| **Seedance 2.0 for hero clips, Kling for stylized, Flow Lite for bulk** | Fits "cinematic" aesthetic expectation; 2K Seedance matches production quality norm of niche |
| **Closing CTA: "Which piece of this story stayed with you? Tell me in the comments."** | Reflection-inviting — fits Need-for-Cognition trait |
