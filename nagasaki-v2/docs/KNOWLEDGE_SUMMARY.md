## Act I — Sakoku & Dejima (1639-1853)

### The sealed country
After the 1637-38 Shimabara Rebellion (a partly-Christian peasant uprising brutally crushed by the Tokugawa shogunate), Japan entered **sakoku** ("locked country") under the 1639 *Sakoku-rei* edicts. From 1639 until 1853, Japanese citizens could not leave the archipelago; foreigners (with narrow exceptions) could not enter. Christianity was proscribed on pain of death. The *fumi-e* ceremony — forcing suspects to trample an image of Christ or the Virgin Mary — became an annual test in villages under suspicion.

### Dejima
One tiny breach remained. **Dejima** (出島, "exit island") — a fan-shaped artificial island in Nagasaki Bay, roughly **120 × 75 m** (~9,000 m² / 2.2 acres), connected to the mainland by one gated bridge — housed the **Dutch East India Company** factory from 1641 onward (relocated from Hirado). Initially built 1634-36 for Portuguese traders, the Portuguese were expelled in 1639 after Shimabara.

For 214 years, Dejima was the single legal point of contact between Japan and the Western world.

### The tsuji — interpreters as smugglers of knowledge
- Dutch merchants were not allowed beyond the island's perimeter except for the annual **hofreis** — a diplomatic trip to Edo to pay tribute to the shōgun.
- Japanese civilians were not allowed onto Dejima without permission.
- The traffic between worlds ran through **oranda tsūji** (阿蘭陀通詞) — hereditary Japanese interpreters of Dutch.
- The tsuji had dual lives: official roles (contract translation, customs brokerage) and unofficial ones (smuggling books, instruments, medical tracts past shogunal censors).
- From this channel grew **rangaku** (蘭学, "Dutch learning") — the body of Western knowledge that entered Japan in translation: Copernican astronomy, anatomy (*Kaitai Shinsho* 1774, first Japanese translation of a European anatomy textbook), gunnery, cartography, electricity (Hiraga Gennai's *elekiteru* friction generator, 1776), vaccination against smallpox (1824).

### Nagasaki the watched city
Nagasaki itself was uniquely Catholic in origin — founded as a trading port by Jesuits + the Ōmura daimyō family in 1571. After the Christian ban it was placed under direct shogunal control. A network of informers, mandatory temple registration (*terauke*), and annual *fumi-e* tests made it the most tightly-watched city in Japan. This is where the **kakure kirishitan** (hidden Christians) adapted to survive.

### Kakure kirishitan practices
Without priests (all expelled or executed by 1644), hidden Christians developed a syncretic cover:
- Prayers in corrupted Latin/Portuguese handed down orally (e.g. the *Orasho*).
- Statues of the Virgin Mary disguised as Buddhist *Kannon* goddess figures (*Maria Kannon*).
- Leadership by laypeople — *mizukata* (water-giver/baptizer), *chōkata* (prayer-leader), *kikikata* (listener-confessor).
- Calendar maintained in secret: Easter, Christmas, feasts of saints tracked by lunar observation.
- Baptism continued uninterrupted; children of communities in Urakami, Sotome, Ikitsuki, and Goto islands remained nominally Christian for **≈ 250 years**.

## Act II — Coming out of the shadows (1865-1944)

### 17 March 1865 — Ōura Cathedral
Two years after the forced end of sakoku (Perry's 1853 opening + 1858 treaties), France negotiated permission to build a Catholic church in Nagasaki for foreign residents. **Ōura Cathedral** (正式名 Nihon Nijūroku Seijin Junkyōsha Tenshudō, "Church of the Twenty-Six Martyrs of Japan") opened in early 1865, dedicated to the 26 Christians crucified in Nagasaki in 1597.

On **17 March 1865**, a group of about 15 villagers from Urakami walked into the new church. Their leader, a woman named **Isabelina Yuri Sugimoto** (according to local tradition; some sources name an older male leader), approached French priest **Bernard Thaddée Petitjean** and whispered the phrase that has since become the birth line of the **"Shinto no Hakken"** (信徒発見, "Discovery of the Faithful"):

> *"The hearts of all of us here are the same as yours."*

Then, in Japanese:

> *"Santa Maria no go-zō wa doko?"* — "Where is the statue of Holy Mary?"

Petitjean led them to a Virgin Mary statue. They prayed. In the following weeks he learned: the community had preserved Catholicism for 250 years through oral transmission, maintained baptism, recognized the Pope, observed Easter.

Some 30,000 kakure kirishitan in Nagasaki region revealed themselves in the months following.

### Urakami-kuzure IV (Fourth Urakami Persecution), 1867-1873
The shogunate (still legally in power for another year) responded with the **fourth** major persecution of Urakami Christians — the three earlier (1790, 1839, 1856) had been contained. This one was the largest and the final.
- **3,394 Christians** arrested, from the villages of Urakami Yamazato.
- Exiled to **20 feudal domains** across Japan to be "re-educated" via torture, starvation, and forced labor.
- **662 died** in exile (disease, abuse, starvation).
- Torture techniques documented: suspension in pits (*tsurushi*), water torture, cold exposure, beatings, deprivation of food and shelter.
- The early Meiji government, initially continuing Tokugawa anti-Christian policy, came under heavy Western diplomatic pressure (Iwakura Mission 1871-73 faced hostile receptions in Europe/US over this). The edict boards prohibiting Christianity were removed in February 1873.
- Exiles returned to Urakami. Reconstruction of the community began.

### Urakami Cathedral, 1895-1914
The freed community began construction of a brick cathedral in the village of Urakami in 1895. Built largely by the parishioners themselves on a site that had been the *chō-yakuba* (district office) where many of them had been forced to trample *fumi-e* images during the persecutions. Dedicated 1914 as **Urakami Cathedral** (浦上天主堂), also called Saint Mary's Cathedral.

- Twin-towered neo-Romanesque design, red brick.
- Largest Christian church in East Asia at time of completion.
- ≈ 12,000 parishioners by the 1940s — Japan's largest Catholic congregation, all descended from the kakure kirishitan revealed in 1865.

## Act III — 9 August 1945, 11:02 JST

### The mission
Nagasaki was not the primary target. **Kokura** was primary, **Nagasaki** secondary. On the morning of 9 August 1945, the B-29 *Bockscar* — piloted by Major Charles Sweeney — circled Kokura for nearly an hour but the target was obscured by haze and smoke (possibly from firebombing of neighboring Yahata the night before). With fuel limits forcing a decision, Sweeney diverted to Nagasaki. Nagasaki was also mostly cloud-covered, but a brief opening in the clouds let the bombardier **Kermit Beahan** visually confirm a target. He aimed for the Mitsubishi Steel & Arms Works in the **Urakami valley** — ≈ 3 km northwest of the originally-intended city-center aim point.

### The detonation
- **Time:** 11:02 local (JST).
- **Altitude:** ≈ **503 m (1,650 ft)** above ground.
- **Weapon:** *Fat Man* — plutonium implosion device.
- **Yield:** ≈ **21 kt TNT-equivalent** (40-50% more than Hiroshima's Little Boy).
- **Hypocenter:** directly over **Matsuyama-chō** — about **500 m north of Urakami Cathedral**.

### 43 seconds
From the moment the cathedral bells could no longer be heard to the moment of total destruction: approximately the time it takes to say a single *Pater Noster*.

- The cathedral's stone walls were reduced to fragments; the two bell towers collapsed, one of the bells (the "angelus bell") was found 35 m away, partially melted.
- Mass was being prepared for the Feast of the Assumption (15 August). Estimates: **20-30 parishioners** inside the cathedral at the moment, including priests hearing confession. All killed.
- Of the ≈ **12,000 Christians** in the Urakami valley, an estimated **8,500+** were killed by the bomb — roughly 70% of the community that had survived 250 years of persecution.
- Two-thirds of all Christian deaths of the atomic bombings occurred in that single congregation.

### Nagai Takashi
Our narrator-anchor is modelled on the historical **Nagai Takashi** (永井 隆, 1908-1951).
- Christian convert (baptized 1934) and professor of radiology at **Nagasaki Medical College**, located ~700 m from the hypocenter.
- Already terminally ill with leukemia (occupational exposure from years of X-ray work) at the time of the bombing.
- Survived the blast at his office 700 m from hypocenter; his wife **Midori** was killed at home in Urakami — he later found her rosary in the ashes of the kitchen.
- Spent his remaining years writing from a 2-tatami hermitage hut ("Nyokodō" — named after "love thy neighbor as thyself"), producing:
  - *Nagasaki no Kane* (長崎の鐘, "The Bells of Nagasaki") — survivor's memoir, published 1949 with a chapter the Occupation censors cut (restored in later editions). Became a national bestseller; inspired a film and a song.
  - *Kono Ko o Nokoshite* ("Leaving These Children Behind") — letter to his son and daughter about living on after loss.
  - *Rosario no Kusari* ("The Chain of the Rosary").
- Died May 1951 of leukemia. ≈ 20,000 attended his funeral. Beatification cause opened.

Nagai's interpretive frame — controversial in some Japanese circles but central to Urakami Catholic memory — was that the bombing had been a *hansai* (燔祭, "burnt offering") that the suffering of Urakami Christians had been offered for the end of the war. This is theologically contested (some Nagasaki Catholics and many non-Catholics reject the frame as implicit justification for US policy). **Our script uses Nagai as a human portal, not as moral doctrine.**

## Visual motifs across the three eras

**Unifying image: the same valley, three times.**
- Act I: A green rural valley with *washi* paper-screen houses, kilns, the glint of a Dutch East India lantern behind Dejima's wall, a tsuji in indigo carrying a sealed scroll at dawn.
- Act II: The same valley now with brick cathedral towers rising, industrial smokestacks of Mitsubishi Steel in the middle distance, processions of white-clad parishioners.
- Act III: The same valley after the flash. Standing walls of brick, the broken statue of the Virgin (the *hibaku Maria* — her scorched wooden head survived and is preserved today), black rain falling on what was a congregation.

**Recurring objects that bridge time:**
1. **The bell** — cast for Urakami 1914, partially melted 1945, retrieved and rung annually at the 9 August memorial.
2. **The rosary** — from the kakure kirishitan era (hidden, strung with knotted cord) to Midori Nagai's (found in ash).
3. **The statue of Mary** — the Ōura "Santa Maria" the villagers asked for in 1865, and the scorched *hibaku Maria* head pulled from the rubble in 1945.
4. **The harbor** — Dejima's fan-shape, now a land-locked monument (the bay was filled in during Meiji-era industrialization).
