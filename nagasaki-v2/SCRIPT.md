> In 1865, a group of Japanese peasants walked into a newly-built Catholic church in Nagasaki and confessed a secret their families had protected for two hundred and fifty years. ## Scene 2 — Context and first CTA
**Time:** 0:15-1:00 | **Narration:** ~100 words | **Retention:** Curiosity reinforcement + first Like/Subscribe CTA

> This is the story of the Urakami valley — a stretch of farmland in northwestern Nagasaki that has lived three separate lives. *[storytelling tone]* In the first, it was a quiet corner of a sealed empire, where Japan spoke to the outside world through a single keyhole. In the second, the keyhole opened — and something centuries old walked through it. In the third, the third sun of the twentieth century rose over its parish church, at eleven oh two on a summer morning.
>
> Before we go further — ⟨CTA-1⟩ if you want to know how a secret faith survives ten generations, and what happens to it when it meets an atomic weapon, subscribe. The bells of Nagasaki deserve the hour I'm going to spend telling you this.

**Visual motif:** slow dolly over Urakami valley aerial, three overlapping title cards ("1639", "1865", "1945") fading in sequence. CTA overlay subtle lower-third.

## Scene 3 — Sakoku & Dejima
**Time:** 1:00-2:00 | **Narration:** ~145 words | **Retention:** Narrative transportation + ⟨OL1⟩ plant

> Imagine you are standing at the edge of a fan-shaped island made of packed earth. It is the year sixteen forty-five. The island is one hundred and twenty metres long, seventy-five metres wide — about the size of a city block. Behind you, Nagasaki Bay. In front of you, a single wooden bridge with a gate. On the other side of the gate, the empire of Japan — twenty-seven million people who, by imperial edict, may not cross to where you are standing.
>
> Your name is lost to history. You are an *oranda tsūji* — a Dutch interpreter, born of a Japanese mother and trained since boyhood in a language no one in your country may legally learn. ⟨OL1⟩ Every morning you walk across this bridge with a sealed lacquer box. Officially, it holds translated invoices. Unofficially, sometimes, it holds something else: an anatomical diagram. A star chart. A translation of a Bible.

**Visual motif:** Dejima dawn, warm lantern glow, single figure in indigo crossing wooden bridge, sealed box in hand. Cut to interior of tsuji's home where translated Dutch scientific drawings are hidden under floorboards.

## Scene 4 — 250-year secret
**Time:** 2:00-3:30 | **Narration:** ~215 words | **Retention:** Zeigarnik + pattern interrupts + ⟨CTA-2⟩

> Christianity had come to Japan in fifteen forty-nine, with a Jesuit named Francis Xavier. For seventy years, it flourished. At its peak, more than three hundred thousand Japanese were baptized — half of them in and around the port you see across the water.
>
> Then the shogunate saw a threat. In sixteen fourteen, Christianity was banned. In sixteen thirty-seven, thirty-seven thousand peasants — many of them Christian — rose up at Shimabara and were crushed, their bodies thrown into the Ariake Sea. By sixteen forty-four, every foreign priest in Japan was either dead or deported.
>
> And yet, in the villages around Urakami, something did not stop.
>
> Parents baptized their own children, using rainwater and whispered Portuguese. Grandmothers memorized Latin prayers they had never learned to read. Mary was disguised as the Buddhist goddess Kannon — *Maria Kannon* — so her face could sit on a household altar without accusation. A prayer called the *Orasho*, handed down by ear for two hundred and fifty years, mutated into something half-Portuguese, half-Japanese, half-song.
>
> ⟨CTA-2⟩ Stop and think about that for a second. How do you keep a faith alive for ten generations with no priests, no printed books, no public ritual? If you've ever kept a family tradition going through hard times, tell me in the comments — what did it take?

**Visual motif:** montage of hidden objects — Maria Kannon statue, knotted-cord rosary inside a bamboo tube, a fumi-e plate laid on wooden planks, women whispering over an infant at night. Pattern-interrupt cuts every 25-30s (fumi-e close-up at 2:30; Maria Kannon reveal at 2:55). CTA text lower-third at 3:15.

## Scene 5 — Ōura 1865 and Urakamikuzure
**Time:** 3:30-5:00 | **Narration:** ~200 words | **Retention:** ⟨OL1⟩ payoff + ⟨OL2⟩ plant

> On the seventeenth of March, eighteen sixty-five, a French priest named Bernard Petitjean was alone in a new church on a hill above the harbor. The church was called Ōura Cathedral — built for foreign residents, because Japan had just, reluctantly, opened. He heard footsteps behind him. Fifteen Japanese peasants. A woman stepped forward and whispered two lines. The first, in broken Latin. The second, in Japanese.
>
> *[cinematic tone]* "Santa Maria no go-zō wa doko?" — *Where is the statue of Holy Mary?*
>
> ⟨OL1⟩ *The keyhole had opened. Something two hundred and fifty years old had just walked through it.*
>
> Within weeks, thirty thousand hidden Christians in the region revealed themselves. And the dying shogunate, one last time, chose persecution. *[storytelling tone]* In what history calls the Fourth Urakami Persecution, three thousand three hundred and ninety-four villagers were arrested and exiled to twenty feudal domains to be tortured back into silence. Six hundred and sixty-two died in exile.
>
> Six years later the edicts were repealed. The survivors walked home to Urakami. Over the next thirty-nine years, with their own hands, they built a brick cathedral on the land where they had been forced to trample on the face of Christ. ⟨OL2⟩ They had come out of hiding. The world they emerged into was eighty years away from falling on them.

**Visual motif:** Ōura 1865 — low-angle, single candle, whispered exchange with Petitjean. Cut to period woodcuts of Urakamikuzure exile. Time-lapse of Urakami Cathedral rising 1895-1914. Slow pull back to show the cathedral standing tall over the valley — same valley from Act I opening, 250 years later.

## Scene 6 — 9 August 1945, 11:02
**Time:** 5:00-7:30 | **Narration:** ~310 words | **Retention:** Emotional arc peak + ⟨CTA-3⟩ + ⟨OL2⟩ payoff

> By the summer of nineteen forty-five, Urakami held Japan's largest Catholic congregation — twelve thousand people — all of them, every one, descended from the fifteen who whispered to Petitjean eighty years earlier. They worshipped at Urakami Cathedral. It was, at that time, the largest Christian church in East Asia.
>
> Among them was Takashi Nagai. Radiologist at Nagasaki Medical College. Christian convert, baptized eleven years earlier. Dying of leukemia from a decade of X-ray exposure. Husband of Midori, also Christian, also of Urakami blood. They had two young children.
>
> At eleven oh one on the ninth of August, Midori was home in Urakami, finishing rosary morning prayer in the kitchen.
>
> At eleven oh one on the ninth of August, a B-twenty-nine named Bockscar broke out of cloud cover above Nagasaki. Its primary target — the city of Kokura — had been obscured by haze. The bombardier, Kermit Beahan, had thirty seconds of visual window on the secondary target. He chose the valley with the most distinct landmark visible through the clouds: a twin-towered brick cathedral.
>
> ⟨CTA-3⟩ *[quiet tone]* What comes next is the hardest part of the story. If you've made it here, like this and stay with me — it matters that people hear it.
>
> At eleven oh two, Fat Man — twenty-one kilotons of plutonium — detonated five hundred and three metres above Matsuyama-chō. The hypocentre was five hundred metres from the cathedral.
>
> ⟨OL2⟩ *The second sun had opened. The forty-three seconds had begun.*
>
> The cathedral's stone walls became gravel. The two bell towers fell. One bell was found thirty-five metres from the foundation, half-melted. Mass was being prepared for the Feast of the Assumption. Twenty-something parishioners inside the walls — priests hearing confession — were killed in an instant. Of the twelve thousand Urakami Christians, more than eight and a half thousand were dead.
>
> Takashi Nagai, at the medical college seven hundred metres from the hypocentre, survived, buried in rubble. He walked home through a firestorm to find his kitchen — and the rosary Midori had been holding, fused to the bone of her hand.

**Visual motif:** clock close-up ticking toward 11:02 (Shepard-tone audio bed). Cut to Bockscar over clouds — quick, 10s total. At 11:02: white frame, single bell chime. Ruins emerge from white — the hibaku Maria head (scorched wooden Virgin), the melted bell, the shattered brick. Silent tracking shot through the gutted cathedral interior. Nagai walking home. Close-up on rosary in ash.

## Scene 7 — Conclusion + closing CTA
**Time:** 7:30-9:00 | **Narration:** ~120 words | **Retention:** ⟨OL2⟩/⟨OL3⟩ full payoff + closing question CTA

> Takashi Nagai survived four more years. From a two-tatami hut he called Nyokodō — *love thy neighbor as thyself* — he wrote *The Bells of Nagasaki*. He died in nineteen fifty-one. Twenty thousand people came to his funeral.
>
> The half-melted bell was pulled from the rubble. A new cathedral was raised on the same foundation in nineteen fifty-nine. The bell rings today, every year, on the ninth of August at eleven oh two.
>
> ⟨OL3⟩ *The bell has now rung on three continents of time. It was cast in nineteen fourteen. It melted in nineteen forty-five. It rings again today. And every year it rings for a community that refused, three times, to disappear.*
>
> ⟨CTA-4 — closing question⟩ Which piece of this story stayed with you the most — the two hundred and fifty years of hiding, the forty-three seconds, or the bell that still rings? Tell me in the comments below. *[soft cinematic tone]* Next video, we return to the sea — to another port city that remembers.

**Visual motif:** Nagai in Nyokodō hut, writing; his funeral procession; intercut with modern-day Urakami Cathedral at dawn, the recovered bell being struck slowly, the camera holding on the bell's vibration. Final frame: still valley, single wisp of incense, 11:02 on a modern wristwatch. Fade.
