## 1. Scene Breakdown

16 scenes, organized into 4 acts across 3 eras plus framing. Total target: 15:30-17:00.

### Act I — Hook & Foundation

| Scene | Time | Title | Content Summary | Character Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **01** | 0:00-0:15 | Cold Hook | Double Hook: modern Galata Bridge fisherman at dawn → camera reveals 2,700-year skyline. Narrator: "This man is doing exactly what men have done on this shore for twenty-seven centuries." | None (visual) |
| **02** | 0:15-2:00 | Context & Curiosity Gap | Establish the thesis: Istanbul is the only city that became capital of three completely different civilizations. Bosphorus as geographic destiny. Plant L1 ("three times conquered") and L2 ("why every empire needed this strait"). End with: "It all started with a fisherman." | None (narrator) |

### Act II — Era 1: Greek Byzantion (657 BC - 330 AD)

| Scene | Time | Title | Content Summary | Character Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **03** | 2:00-3:15 | The Fisherman's Harbor | 657 BC — Greek colonist follows the current into the Golden Horn. Fish are pushed into the natural harbor by the Bosphorus current. Introduce the fisherman: morning ritual, mending nets, launching his boat. Sensory details: salt air, wooden hull, dawn light on water. | Greek Fisherman |
| **04** | 3:15-4:30 | Byzantion Takes Root | The fishing settlement grows — a natural harbor, a defensible peninsula, freshwater springs. Other colonists arrive. The fisherman sees the first stone walls go up. Trade begins — grain ships from the Black Sea pass through the strait. | Greek Fisherman (peripheral) |
| **05** | 4:30-5:30 | The Strait's First Lesson | Persians, Athenians, Romans — everyone who controls the strait controls the grain supply of the Mediterranean. | **06** | 5:30-6:00 | Transition — From Nets to Silk | The harbor that fed a fishing village now feeds an empire. Constantine sees what the fisherman saw — a perfect harbor. 330 AD: Byzantion becomes Constantinople. J-cut: merchant's voice begins over fisherman's harbor. Open loop L4: "The fisherman's harbor would become the richest marketplace on Earth." | Transition |

### Act III — Era 2: Byzantine Constantinople (330 - 1453)

| Scene | Time | Title | Content Summary | Character Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **07** | 6:00-7:15 | The Merchant Arrives | Introduce the silk merchant — a trader at the crossroads of the Silk Road and Mediterranean sea routes. His world: the Mese (main avenue), the forums, the harbors crowded with ships from every known civilization. Sensory: silk texture, market noise, incense. | Byzantine Merchant |
| **08** | 7:15-8:30 | City of the World's Desire | Constantinople at its peak — Hagia Sophia's golden interior, the Hippodrome, the Theodosian Walls. | **09** | 8:30-9:30 | The Weight of Silk and Gold | The merchant's daily life — negotiating with Venetian traders, Chinese silk arriving overland, spices from India by sea. The Bosphorus as bottleneck: every trade route funnels through his city. Resolve L4 (richest marketplace). The Grand Bazaar's predecessor emerges. | Byzantine Merchant |
| **10** | 9:30-10:00 | Transition — The Walls Hold... Until They Don't | 1000 years of attempted sieges — Arabs, Bulgars, Rus, Crusaders (1204 — sack by Fourth Crusade, merchant's world shattered briefly). The walls always hold. Open loop L5: "By 1450, everyone in Europe knew these walls were unbreakable. They were wrong." J-cut: distant cannon sounds over merchant's final scene. | Transition |

### Act IV — Era 3: Ottoman Istanbul (1453 - ~1600)

| Scene | Time | Title | Content Summary | Character Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **11** | 10:00-11:15 | The Third Reinvention | 1453 — the great transformation. Frame NOT as destruction but as reinvention. Sultan Mehmed II enters the city, goes to Hagia Sophia, orders it preserved. The city is renamed, but the strait remains. Resolve L5 (walls breached). Resolve L3 (walls did not last forever). | Historical panorama |
| **12** | 11:15-12:15 | The Craftsman's Workshop | Introduce the Ottoman craftsman — a ceramicist or woodworker in the decades after 1453. He works in a city being rebuilt: mosques rise where churches stood, old Roman columns repurposed in new foundations. His perspective: continuity through change. | Ottoman Craftsman |
| **13** | 12:15-13:30 | Suleymaniye | Suleiman the Magnificent's Istanbul — the craftsman contributes to the great building projects. Suleymaniye Mosque construction: scaffolding, stone dust, the architect Sinan's vision. The craftsman sees a skyline his merchant predecessor would not recognize. Open loop L6: "The craftsman's workshop would outlive empires. It's still there." | Ottoman Craftsman |
| **14** | 13:30-14:00 | The Grand Bazaar Lives | The Grand Bazaar — a living organism that survived all three civilizations. The craftsman sells his wares in the same covered market that the Byzantine merchant walked through. Continuity made tangible. J-cut: modern ship horn over craftsman's scene. | Ottoman Craftsman |

### Act V — Climax & Conclusion

| Scene | Time | Title | Content Summary | Character Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **15** | 14:00-16:00 | The Bosphorus Remembers | Climax — pull back to the eternal thread. The Bosphorus has watched all of this. Same water, same current that pushed fish into the Golden Horn in 657 BC still flows. Resolve L1 ("three times conquered" — now understood), L2 ("why every empire needed this strait" — geographic destiny made visceral). Key insight delivered: "Istanbul is the only city in history that became the capital of three completely different civilizations — and each time, it was the strait that made it inevitable." Match cut: same Bosphorus framing across Greek, Byzantine, Ottoman eras. | All three (echoes) |
| **16** | 16:00-17:00 | Conclusion & Tease | Return to the Galata Bridge fisherman from the hook — full circle. He casts his line into the same current. Resolve L6 (Grand Bazaar still there). Tease next video / future episode. Soft CTA via value proposition. End screen. | Modern fisherman (bookend) |

### Tempo Variation Pattern

The pacing follows a "breathe-build-release" rhythm:

1. **Breathe** (slow, intimate) — character introductions (Scenes 03, 07, 12)
2. **Build** (medium, accelerating) — historical content, stakes rising (Scenes 04-05, 08-09, 13)
3. **Release** (fast or transitional) — era climaxes and transitions (Scenes 06, 10, 14)
4. **Grand breathe** (very slow, deliberate) — final climax and conclusion (Scenes 15-16)

### Prohibited Words — Scriptwriter Must Avoid

The following are banned from the narration script:

- "Hey guys", "welcome back", any greeting variants
- "Subscribe", "like" (in first 60 seconds; minimal use after)
- "Mind-blowing", "insane", "you won't believe"
- "Dark Ages"
- "Exotic", "Oriental", "mysterious East"
- "Discovered by [Europeans]"
- "Massacre", "genocide", "torture" (use contextual alternatives)
- "Fall of Constantinople" (use "transformation", "reinvention", "the city changed hands")

### Character-Driven Narration Checklist

Every scene must pass these tests:
- [ ] Is a named or implied character present, or is their absence narratively motivated?
- [ ] Does the viewer see the city through someone's eyes, not from a textbook?
- [ ] Are sensory details present (what the character sees, hears, touches, smells)?
- [ ] Does the narration use active voice with strong verbs?
- [ ] Is there at least one line that could only be said about THIS city in THIS era?
