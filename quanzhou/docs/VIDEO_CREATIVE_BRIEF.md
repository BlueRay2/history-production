# Video Creative Brief — Quanzhou

**Channel:** Cities Evolution
**Date:** 2026-04-09

---

## Working Title Options

1. **The Chinese Port That Named Satin** — Etymology hook; discovery factor; implies hidden global influence
2. **The Port That Vanished: Quanzhou and the Maritime Silk Road** — Disappearance mystery + prestige keyword
3. **Seven Religions, One Harbor: The Rise and Fall of Quanzhou** — Religious diversity hook; dramatic arc
4. **Zayton: The Port Ibn Battuta Called the Greatest in the World** — Authority quote; unfamiliar name creates curiosity
5. **Before Venice, Before London — The World's Busiest Port Was Chinese** — Competitive framing; challenges Western assumptions

**Recommended:** #1 — "The Chinese Port That Named Satin." Strongest discovery factor. The audience doesn't know the word "satin" comes from China. The title promises a revelation they can verify and repeat.

---

## Logline

A fishing village on China's Fujian coast became the busiest port in the medieval world — home to seven religions, a hundred ships, and the fabric that bears its name — then vanished so completely that most people have never heard of it.

---

## Cold Hook Concept (0:00-0:15)

**Visual:** Extreme wide of Song Dynasty Quanzhou harbor at golden hour — a hundred ships, twin pagodas silhouetted, every flag of the Indian Ocean world. Then match cut to the same bay, empty, silted, quiet.

**Narration:** "You're probably wearing something named after this city right now. Satin — the word comes from Zayton, the Arabic name for a Chinese port that was the busiest in the medieval world for three hundred years. Then it vanished."

**Technique:** Double Hook (visual spectacle + etymology revelation) + In Medias Res (peak city, not chronological start) + Curiosity Gap ("then it vanished").

## Thesis Statement

Quanzhou proves that geography is not destiny — it is opportunity. The same bay that made it the world's busiest port could not protect it from human choices: betrayal, xenophobia, and imperial decree. The city that drew the world to its harbor now survives in a word most people say without knowing where it comes from.

## Visual Style Notes (Quanzhou-specific)

- **Color palette:** Warm taupe, sandy brown, warm umber, cream white — consistent with channel style line
- **Era-specific accents:**
  - Tang: Morning mist, soft greens, red Erythrina flowers
  - Song: Golden light, warm amber, crowded textures
  - Yuan: Richer golds, deeper shadows, cosmopolitan color variety
  - Ming decline: Desaturated, cooler tones creeping in, emptier frames
- **Recurring visual motif:** Quanzhou Bay — same water, different centuries. The bay appears in every era as an anchor. Ships change; water doesn't.
- **Architecture motif:** Kaiyuan Temple twin pagodas — visible in skyline across eras
