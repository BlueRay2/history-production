# Audience Persona — Quanzhou: The Port That Named Satin

**Channel:** Cities Evolution
**Video:** Quanzhou — The Port That Named Satin (~17 min)
**Date:** 2026-04-09

---

## Primary Persona: "The Curious Globalist"

**Name:** Alex (composite)
**Age:** 28-42
**Gender:** 60% male / 40% female
**Location:** US, UK, Canada, Australia, Western Europe; secondary: Southeast Asian diaspora (English-speaking)
**Education:** College-educated; self-directed learner
**Income:** Middle to upper-middle

### Psychographic Profile

**Core identity:** Intellectually curious, values depth over breadth, suspicious of surface-level content. Watches documentaries as a form of active relaxation — lean-back viewing that still feeds the brain. Subscribes to history and culture channels but drops any that feel like "AI slop" or clickbait.

**Relationship with Asia:** Genuine interest without expertise. Knows the Silk Road existed, might know Marco Polo traveled to China, but has never heard of Quanzhou. Likely knows more about European port cities (Venice, Constantinople) than Asian ones. This knowledge gap is the opportunity — the "discovery factor."

**Content consumption pattern:**
- Evening viewing, 7-10 PM local time
- Full-length videos (15-25 min) on history/culture channels
- Algorithm-driven discovery + subscription feed
- Watches 2-3 videos per session
- Pauses to Google interesting claims
- Shares videos that make them feel smarter

**What hooks this person:**
1. "I've never heard of this" — discovery of something significant they didn't know existed
2. Human-scale stories within epic history — not just "the empire rose," but "the merchant weighed silk against gold"
3. Specific, verifiable details — numbers, quotes from primary sources, named artifacts
4. Visual beauty that respects intelligence — cinematic AI footage that doesn't look like a video game
5. Non-Eurocentric framing — they're tired of "Europe discovered everything"

**What loses this person:**
1. AI artifacts on faces/hands — instant credibility loss
2. Recycled Wikipedia narration without original insight
3. "Hey guys welcome back" energy
4. Orientalist framing ("mysterious East," "exotic Orient")
5. Factual errors or unsupported claims
6. Monotone AI voiceover

### Quanzhou-Specific Hooks

**Discovery triggers (things Alex doesn't know):**
- The word "satin" comes from this city's Arabic name (Zayton)
- This port was bigger than any European port for 300+ years
- It had Hindu temples — the only surviving ones in China
- A religion born in Persia (Manichaeism) survived here by disguising itself as Buddhism
- Chinese ship technology was centuries ahead of Europe
- Ibn Battuta called it "the greatest port in the world"

**Emotional entry points:**
- The fisherman watching his quiet bay fill with a hundred ships — the ordinary person inside the epic
- The porcelain merchant who speaks Arabic, Tamil, and Chinese but has never left his city
- Pu Shougeng's betrayal — a man who sold a dynasty to the Mongols
- The Ispah Rebellion destroying centuries of coexistence in a single decade
- The empty harbor after the sea ban — a great city slowly going quiet

### Viewing Expectations for This Video

- Runtime: 17 min feels right — long enough for depth, short enough to finish in one sitting
- Pacing: Measured but not slow; each era should feel distinct
- Tone: BBC documentary warmth, not History Channel bombast
- Visuals: Cinematic, warm palette, period-appropriate, no anachronisms
- Narration: Empathetic documentarian — someone who cares about these people
- Payoff: A genuine insight they can repeat at dinner — "Did you know the word satin comes from a Chinese port city?"

## Secondary Persona: "The Diaspora Connection"

**Profile:** Southeast Asian of Chinese (Hokkien) descent, 25-45, lives in Singapore/Malaysia/Philippines/Indonesia. Speaks English fluently. Knows family came from Fujian but has limited knowledge of the historical context. This video connects their personal heritage to world history.

**Unique hook:** The diaspora section — Quanzhou's people spreading across Southeast Asia — is personally relevant. They may recognize place names, family customs, or religious practices.

**Retention risk:** Lower — personal investment keeps them watching. But they'll be more critical of factual accuracy regarding Chinese culture.
