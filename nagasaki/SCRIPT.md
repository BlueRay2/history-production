## Scene 02 — Three Cities in One Valley (0:33-1:45)
**Duration:** 72s | **Retention:** Curiosity Gap + Thesis Framing + L1/L4 Seed

### Narration
[hushed, reverent] Nagasaki was never one story. It was three cities stacked inside the same harbor. One lived on a fan-shaped island where the Dutch were fenced in and the world was translated through a single guarded bridge. Another survived in the farms and ravines of Urakami [oo-rah-KAH-mee], where prayers were passed from mouth to mouth after every priest had been expelled. The third was the brick Catholic city those villagers built when the ban finally lifted — and the city the bomb found by accident, not intention. (per K.S. §§3.1, 4.2, 5.1-5.2)

[contemplative] So the real questions are not only what happened here, but why it happened here. Why did the bomb fall on this valley instead of downtown Nagasaki? What exactly did those fifteen villagers carry into Ōura [OH-oo-rah] Cathedral in 1865? And why does the name George Zabelka — a Catholic chaplain in an American uniform — belong in the same story as a hidden Christian village on the edge of Japan? (per K.S. §§4.5, 5.7)

[slow] To answer any of that, we have to begin with a prison called Dejima.

## Scene 03 — Dejima, the Guarded Bridge (1:45-2:55)
**Duration:** 70s | **Retention:** Narrative Transportation + Hyper-Specificity + L3 Seed

### Narration
[documentary style] Dejima [deh-JEE-mah] was built in the 1630s as a fan-shaped artificial island, only about one hundred and twenty meters by seventy-five, first to confine the Portuguese and then, after the Shimabara revolt, to contain the Dutch. From 1641 until Perry arrived in 1853, this cramped island was Japan's only continuous European window — two hundred and twelve years of trade, surveillance, and suspicion compressed behind one bridge. (per K.S. §3.1)

[measured] Our guide is an Oranda-tsūji [oh-RAHN-dah TSOO-jee], one of the Japanese interpreters assigned to the Dutch and born into one of Nagasaki's old language families. He translates invoices, arguments, anatomy, and astronomy. He also serves the bakufu as a watcher. On Dejima, language is not culture. Language is border control. (per K.S. §3.3)

[understated] Ships arrive stripped of sails. Priests are gone. Christian books are confiscated. And somewhere beyond the harbor, in villages the Dutch are not meant to see, an older forbidden language is still being whispered. How those prayers survive without books or clergy becomes the valley's deepest secret. (per K.S. §§3.2, 4.2)

## Scene 04 — Knowledge Through a Keyhole (2:55-4:05)
**Duration:** 70s | **Retention:** Pattern Interrupt + Discovery Stack

### Narration
[contemplative] For all its suspicion, Dejima also became an engine of leakage. One anatomical book, one pair of spectacles, one celestial chart, one physician at a time. In 1774, Japanese scholars published *Kaitai Shinsho* after verifying a Dutch anatomy text against a dissected corpse. They were working from tiny vocabularies, paying dearly for pages, and inventing modern medical language almost by hand. (per K.S. §3.4)

[documentary style] That is the irony of Dejima. A place designed to keep Europe out became the place where rangaku — Dutch learning — entered Japan. Siebold taught at Narutaki. Kaempfer observed the island in the 1690s and called it a jail. Our tsūji spends his life carrying modern science across the bridge while the word "Christian" remains dangerous on both sides of it. (per K.S. §§3.2-3.4)

[slow] And once you notice that contrast, Nagasaki changes shape. On the waterfront, forbidden knowledge arrives in crates. In Urakami, forbidden memory survives without paper at all.

## Scene 05 — The Silence Begins (4:05-5:15)
**Duration:** 70s | **Retention:** Stakes Escalation + L3 Reinforcement

### Narration
[grave] The silence did not begin naturally. It was made. In 1614, Tokugawa authorities expelled missionaries and cast Christianity as a threat to the order of the realm. Then came the Shimabara rebellion of 1637 to 1638 — crushed with overwhelming force, helped even by Dutch cannon fire from offshore. After that, Christianity was no longer treated as error. It was treated as sedition. (per K.S. §4.1)

[measured] The state developed a ritual to prove obedience. Fumi-e: bronze images of Christ or Mary carried house to house, stepped on every new year. Outwardly, you trampled. Inwardly, some prayed *konchirisan*, an act of contrition whispered against the gesture. The whole system depended on one fear — that memory might outlive institutions. (per K.S. §§4.1-4.2)

[hushed] Which leaves the essential mystery untouched. If the priests were gone, the books gone, the churches gone, how did the prayers keep their shape for two hundred and fifty years?

## Scene 06 — The Hidden Grammar of Urakami (5:15-6:30)
**Duration:** 75s | **Retention:** L3 Payoff + Sensory Immersion

### Narration
[hushed, reverent] The answer is that the faith changed shape just enough to live. In Urakami, the hidden Christians — *kakure kirishitan* [kah-koo-ray kee-ree-shee-TAHN] — organized themselves around lay roles. One keeper guarded the calendar. One *mizukata* kept the authority to baptize. Another recited the prayers. Sacred images moved between houses in plain boxes, disguised as household things. (per K.S. §4.2)

[warm] The prayers themselves survived as sound. *Orasho* [oh-RAH-shoh] preserved Latin after the meaning had begun to drift: "Ame Maria karassa binno domisu..." Christmas became *Otaiya*. Easter became "the rising of sorrows." A Chinese Guanyin statue could become Maria-Kannon if a family knew how to look at it. (per K.S. §§4.3-4.4)

[measured] Even death was doubled. A Buddhist funeral by day, then the hidden return-prayer by night. That is how the words lived — not unchanged, but unbroken.

## Scene 14 — Bells, Blessings, and the Argument That Followed (13:25-14:45)
**Duration:** 80s | **Retention:** L4 Payoff + Moral Complexity

### Narration
[steady] One of Urakami Cathedral's bells was thrown clear of the tower and buried. Nagai and others dug it out. On Christmas Eve 1945, it rang over the rubble. The rebuilt cathedral was consecrated in 1959 on the same site, because the parish refused to let the place become only ruin. In 2025, the missing second bell was finally recovered and both rang together for the eightieth anniversary. (per K.S. §5.5)

[grave] But memory did not stay simple. In late 1945, Nagai preached a *hansai* theory — the dead of Urakami as a burnt offering that had helped end the war. Some mourners resisted even then. The man who blessed the bomb spent the rest of his life trying to unlearn what he had blessed. (per K.S. §5.7)

## Scene 15 — What Survived (14:45-16:00)
**Duration:** 75s | **Retention:** Closing Synthesis + Resonant Exit

### Narration
[steady] Nagasaki did not simply hide a faith for two hundred and fifty years.
