## 3. Act II — Sakoku & Dejima (1639–1853)

### 3.1 Physical and political setup
- Dejima (出島) — fan-shaped artificial island, **120 m × 75 m** (~9,000 m² / 0.9 ha), commissioned by shōgun Tokugawa Iemitsu in 1634, completed 1636 originally to corral **Portuguese** traders. After the Shimabara Rebellion (1637–38) ended in Portuguese expulsion (1639), the Dutch VOC — Calvinist, non-missionary, commercially focused — were moved from Hirado to Dejima in 1641 and remained Japan's sole European trade partner for **212 years** until Perry's arrival (1853).
- The Dutch were the only Westerners permitted continuous residence in Japan for over two centuries. The entire window of Western knowledge into Tokugawa Japan — astronomy, anatomy, maps, firearms doctrine, optics — flowed through one guarded wooden bridge in Nagasaki Harbor.

### 3.2 Daily life on Dejima (compressible to 90 seconds of script)
- **10–15 Dutchmen** at any time (*opperhoofd*/captain, deputy, physician, bookkeeper, carpenter, cook + Javanese servants). Watched continuously by: ~50 Japanese gate-guards, an *otona* supervisor, **~150 tsūji interpreters**, all paid by the VOC.
- Ships' **sails were physically removed** by the Nagasaki *bugyō*'s men and stored ashore until departure was authorized — mechanical guarantee of non-escape.
- Women permitted: only *Oranda-yuki* courtesans from the Maruyama pleasure district (**766 women across 74 brothels in 1680**, peaking at 1,443 in 1692).
- Religious books, weapons, crucifixes confiscated on arrival.
- Engelbert Kaempfer's 1692 verdict (direct quotation, usable as cold-open voiceover): *"They… treat us not like honest men, but like criminals, traitors, spies, prisoners… hostages of the Shogun. This jail goes by the name of Dejima."*

### 3.3 The tsūji (通詞) — character anchor candidate for act II
- Hereditary interpreter families — ~30 dynasties, principal lineages **Motoki, Yoshio, Narabayashi, Nishi**. Total interpreter personnel ~150 at peak.
- Double role: commercial translator AND bakufu intelligence officer. Mistranslation = career death. **Yoshio Kōzaemon, 1790**: stripped of inspector post + 5 years' house arrest over a mistranslation in a camphor export dispute.
- **Nishi Kichijurō** registered as pupil interpreter in **1839 at age four** — the youngest tsūji in Japanese history. Role was dynastic, not aptitude-based.
- Tsūji doubled as scholars — **Motoki Yoshinaga** (1735–1794) translated Copernican astronomy; Yoshio Kōgyū collaborated with opperhoofd Titsingh on European medical texts.
- Takahashi Kageyasu (bakufu chief astronomer) **died in prison** awaiting a death sentence after the **1828 Siebold Incident** — he had traded a map of Japan to Siebold for Dutch scientific books.

### 3.4 Rangaku and the smuggling of knowledge
- ***Kaitai Shinsho*** (*New Book on Anatomy*), 1774, by Sugita Genpaku and Maeno Ryōtaku — translation of the Dutch *Ontleedkundige Tafelen* (itself translating J. A. Kulmus's *Tabulae Anatomicae*). They verified accuracy by bribing an executioner to compare a dismembered corpse against the plates in 1771. Foundational text of Japanese Western medicine.
- Maeno Ryōtaku worked from a **~600-word Dutch vocabulary**; a single anatomical plate could cost a samurai's annual stipend.
- VOC sold **>10,000 foreign books** to Japanese scholars late 18th–early 19th century.
- **Philipp Franz von Siebold** (Dejima 1823–29, 1859–62) — opened **Narutaki-juku** (1824), Japan's first formal Western-style school, trained ~50 students including Takano Chōei.

### 3.5 Trade goods (visual references for clip prompts)
- **IN (Dutch → Japan):** raw silk (early), Javanese sugar (later dominant), cotton, *materia medica*, deerskins/sharkskin from Formosa, scientific instruments, books, glass, wool, spices, exotic animals.
- **OUT (Japan → Dutch):** silver/gold (dominant early), **copper (the post-1668 staple)**, camphor, Arita/Imari porcelain, lacquerware, rice.

### 3.6 Satin-tier surprises (act II)
- **The only Dutch flag on Earth, 1810–1815.** When Napoleon annexed the Netherlands and Britain captured Java, opperhoofd **Hendrik Doeff** refused to lower Dejima's flag. For five years, this 120-m fan of reclaimed land in Nagasaki Harbor was the sole surviving scrap of Dutch sovereignty on the planet.
- **The Dead Dutchmen Problem.** 1641–1655, Europeans who died on Dejima were **dumped at sea at night** — Christian graves were forbidden on Japanese soil. From 1655, Dutch corpses were quietly rowed across the bay to **Inasa** cemetery, with Japanese authorities politely pretending not to notice.
- **The annual hofreis (Edo-sanpu) humiliation.** The opperhoofd crawled on hands into the shogunal audience hall. Under Tsunayoshi, Dutchmen were commanded to **sing and dance for the shōgun's amusement** after the formal audience (Kaempfer, 1691).

## 4. Act III — Kakure Kirishitan, Coming-Out, Fourth Persecution (1614 → 1873 → 1944)

### 4.1 The 250-year suppression (compressed backstory)
- **Bateren-tsuihō-no-fumi** (Statement Expelling the Padres) — drafted in a single night by Zen monk Ishin Sūden, signed by Tokugawa Hidetada, dated **31 January 1614** (Gregorian). Opens: *"Japan has been from its origins the land of the gods."* Expelled 98 of 145 missionaries, 350 Kirishitan families.
- **Shimabara Rebellion, 17 Dec 1637 – 15 Apr 1638.** Catholic peasants + rōnin under 16-year-old **Amakusa Shirō** rise against daimyō Matsukura over taxation + anti-Christian policy. Shogunate mobilizes **~125,000 troops** (with **Dutch VOC ship-based cannon** under Couckebacker firing ~426 rounds at the rebels — the only time a European navy shelled Japanese Christians at the shōgun's request) against ~27,000 rebels at Hara Castle. **~37,000 rebels and sympathizers executed.** Portuguese expelled; Christianity treated as existential sedition thereafter.
- **Fumi-e** (*picture-trampling*) — bronze plaques of Jesus or Mary, ~30 cm long, carried house-to-house in wooden boxes. Instituted in Nagasaki **1629**, enforced nationwide post-Shimabara. Ritualized to **the 4th day of the 1st lunar month** (early January New Year). Every resident — commoner, samurai, Buddhist priest — treads the image annually. Practice formally ended **13 April 1858** under Ansei treaty pressure.
- **26 Martyrs of Nishizaka**, 5 February 1597 — six European Franciscans, three Japanese Jesuits (incl. **Paul Miki**, b. ~1564, son of samurai Miki Handayū — first Japanese to enter a religious order), 17 Japanese Franciscan tertiaries. Paraded ~1,000 km from Kyoto/Osaka over 27 winter days with left-ear lobes sliced off. The three youngest — **Luis Ibaraki (12), Antonio Dainan (13), Thomas Kozaki (14)** — sang the Pater Noster from the oxcart. Tied (not nailed) to 26 crosses with iron bands, pierced simultaneously by two executioners. **Canonized by Pius IX on 8 June 1862** — three years before Petitjean's encounter.

### 4.2 Urakami — the kakure heartland
- The valley north of Nagasaki was the densest hidden-Christian stronghold. Scholars estimate **20,000–30,000 hidden Christians** across the greater Nagasaki region by the mid-19th century, with Urakami as the core.
- Three-officer village cell structure:
  - **Chōkata** (帳方) guards the secret liturgical calendar (*Bastian-goyomi*, named after catechist Bastian).
  - **Mizukata** (水方, "the one with water") performs baptism — the only sacrament a layman can validly confect, which is why it outlasted the priests.
  - **Kikiyaku / shukuro** — assistant / reciter.
  - Sacred effigies rotate between officers' houses in lacquered boxes disguised as family altar pieces.
- **Double funeral.** Buddhist priest conducts public rites + gives *kaimyō* posthumous name. After he leaves, the mizukata returns at night and performs *kaeshi-orasho* ("return-prayer"), reversing the Buddhist ceremony and restoring Christian identity before burial.
- **Konchirisan** (from Portuguese *contrição*, "contrition") — the prayer recited inwardly while performing fumi-e outwardly, absolving the act.

### 4.3 Orasho — Latin fossilized through 250 years of oral transmission
- Ave Maria fragment preserved as: *"Ame Maria karassa binno domisu terikobintsu"* (from Latin *"Ave Maria, gratia plena, Dominus tecum, benedicta…"*).
- Word *Eucharistia* drifted through the oral chain: **Yokashichi → Yokanoshichi → Yokashichiya** → eventually written **八日の七夜** ("seventh night of the eighth day"), with the original meaning completely lost.
- **Otaiya** = Christmas. **Kanashimi no agari** ("rising of sorrows") = Easter. *Kontasu* (from Portuguese *contas*) = rosary.

### 4.4 Maria-Kannon — the visual disguise
- Chinese *blanc-de-Chine* (Dehua) white porcelain statues of **Songzi Guanyin** (child-giving form), 20–40 cm tall, imported through Nagasaki. A cross incised into the base, tucked into robe folds, or a tiny crucifix sealed inside a hollow body cavity.
- One documented Edo example (c.1714, Kawaguchi): Maria-Kannon **nested inside a hollow Amida Buddha statue** — disguise within disguise.

### 4.5 The 1865 coming-out — script anchor scene
- **Ōura Cathedral** dedicated **19 February 1865** by the MEP (Société des Missions Étrangères de Paris), to the memory of the 26 Martyrs (canonized 1862).
- **17 March 1865** — roughly a month later, Father **Bernard-Thadée Petitjean** is praying at the altar. Fifteen villagers from Urakami approach.
- **Sugimoto Yuri** (baptismal name **Isabelina**, 1813–1893, age 52 at the moment) whispers through the grille: *"Watashi-domo no mune, anata to onaji"* ("Our breast is the same as yours"). She asks: *"Santa Maria no go-zō wa doko?"* ("Where is the statue of Holy Mary?").
- Petitjean leads them to a Marian image; they weep. Within weeks, messengers arrive from Gotō, Sotome, Hirado. Ultimately **~30,000** hidden Christians surface.
- **Narrative key:** In 250 years of underground transmission, Marian devotion had outgrown every other part of the faith — Mary was the one who could hide as Kannon. The first words after 250 years of silence were a request to see Mary.

### 4.6 The Fourth Urakami Persecution (浦上四番崩れ, 1867–1873)
- July 1867: community refuses Buddhist funeral rites. Initial 68 arrests → 83.
- **7 April 1868** — Meiji government, despite the Restoration, **reaffirms the Christian ban**.
- **3,414 men, women, children exiled** to 20 feudal domains: Kanazawa 500, Satsuma 375, Tsuwano 160, Okayama/Tsurushima 117, etc.
- Documented torture methods: water, snow, ice, fire, starvation, boxing (*hako-zeme*), crucifixion. Lay leader: **Takagi Senemon**.
- **~660–680 died** (~20% mortality).
- Ban lifted **24 February 1873** under foreign diplomatic pressure (the Iwakura Mission had been rebuffed in Europe over it). **~1,900 survivors** returned to Urakami.
- Urakami Cathedral construction begun 1895 on the very plot where the *fumi-e* interrogations had been held; main structure completed **1914**; **bell towers finished 1925**. At that point, **largest Christian building in the Asia-Pacific**. Built brick-by-brick by the kakure descendants themselves — funded over 30 years — as a monument to their survival.

### 4.7 Satin-tier surprises (act III)
- **The canonization and the confession line up eerily.** Rome canonized the 26 Martyrs on 8 June 1862. Ōura Cathedral dedicated to those martyrs on 19 February 1865. In March 1865 the martyrs' spiritual descendants walked out of 250 years of silence and met the cathedral built for them. One of the most cinematically precise coincidences in modern church history.
- **The cathedral sat on the ground of the fumi-e.** Urakami Cathedral was built where, for 250 years, villagers had been forced to trample images of Christ — by the very descendants of those trampling.
- **Kakure vs Hanare.** After 1873, those who rejoined Rome kept the label *Kakure Kirishitan* loosely. Those who refused — either because 250 years of oral drift had rendered their practice unrecognizable, or out of fidelity to ancestral form — are now called *Hanare Kirishitan*. They survive today mostly on Gotō (Hisaka, Narushima) and in Sotome hamlets.

## 5. Act IV — 9 August 1945, 11:02 JST

### 5.1 The target politics
- **10–11 May 1945 Los Alamos Target Committee.** Priority list: Kyoto #1, Hiroshima, Yokohama, Kokura Arsenal. **Nagasaki not on the original list.**
- **Secretary of War Henry L. Stimson personally removes Kyoto** from the list over General Groves' objections, citing cultural-religious significance and the geopolitical risk of permanent Japanese alienation.
- Nagasaki added to the list only in **late July 1945**, as backup to Kokura.
- **Kokura weather diversion, 9 Aug 1945.** *Bockscar* (pilot Maj. **Charles W. Sweeney**) makes three bomb runs over Kokura Arsenal, finds ~70% cloud/smoke cover (smoke drifting from prior night's firebombing of nearby Yahata), cannot get a visual fix, and with one fuel transfer pump dead turns for the secondary. Over Nagasaki, a single break in the clouds gives bombardier **Capt. Kermit K. Beahan** — it was his 27th birthday — his aim point.

### 5.2 Fat Man and the detonation
- **Plutonium implosion design** (6.2 kg Pu core), different from Little Boy's uranium gun-type. Yield ~21 kilotons.
- **11:02 JST, 9 August 1945.** Detonation at ~**503 m altitude** above **Matsuyama-chō**, in the **Urakami valley** — roughly **3 km northwest** of the intended downtown aim point. The bomb missed its target and landed over the Christian quarter.
- The 2.3-by-1.9-mile Urakami "bowl" concentrated blast damage on the Christian community; hills shielded much of central Nagasaki.

### 5.3 Urakami Cathedral in the blast
- **~500 m from hypocenter.** Two priests were hearing confessions inside the cathedral at 11:02 — the congregation was preparing for the **15 August Feast of the Assumption**. Fr. **Saburo Nishida** and all present inside were killed.
- **Casualty range:** Urakami parish ~**12,000** in 1945. Catholic dead estimated **8,500 (Nagai's own figure, Japanese-origin sources) to 9,600 (higher figure in Anglo-Catholic sources)**. Present on screen as "**roughly 8,500 of the parish's 12,000 members**" and credit Nagai, OR present the range. Total Nagasaki deaths by end of 1945: **70,000–80,000**.

### 5.4 Nagai Takashi (1908–1951) — act IV character anchor
- Radiologist at Nagasaki Medical College (Urakami), Catholic convert through his future wife's family. **Already dying of chronic radiation leukemia** from pre-war X-ray work when the bomb fell.
- At 11:02 he was at a darkened X-ray reading desk in the Medical College's radium lab, ~700 m from hypocenter. Survived but critically wounded.
- Wrote *Nagasaki no Kane* (*The Bells of Nagasaki*, completed Aug 1946, published 1949 after occupation censors cleared it). Medical College lost ~**807 faculty and students**.
- His wife **Midori Nagai** killed instantly in their kitchen ~**700 m from hypocenter**. Days later, Nagai recovered "**a bucket-full of soft ashes**" and, in the bones of her hand, **the melted remains of her rosary**. This scene is the single strongest single-image payoff that binds 1865 to 1945 — the same devotional object the kakure had hidden for seven generations, recovered from atomic ash.

### 5.5 The Angelus bell (reconstruction and coda)
- One of Urakami Cathedral's twin bells was blown clear of the belfry and buried. Nagai and volunteers unearthed it.
- **Rung on Christmas Eve 1945** — four months after the bomb.
- Reinforced-concrete replacement cathedral consecrated **1959 on the same site** (the parish insisted, against city plans to preserve the ruins as peace memorial). Brick-tile skin restoring original appearance added 1980.
- For decades one tower rang, the other stood silent. **The missing second bell was finally recovered and both rang together for the 80th anniversary in 2025.** Direct audible throughline from the 1865 discovery to the present — a closing image if wanted.

### 5.6 The theological aftershock — hansai-setsu
- In his **November 1945 funeral oration** for the Urakami dead, Nagai framed the destruction as a providential *hansai* (燔祭, "burnt-offering theory") — a pure Catholic sacrifice that ended the war.
- Embraced by Cold-War-era Japanese Catholics. Bitterly criticized from the 1970s by hibakusha poet **Yamada Kan** and others as theologizing away political responsibility, privileging Urakami Catholics over the broader hibakusha community, and muting anti-nuclear accountability.
- Some mourners reportedly shouted Nagai down at the first funeral where he preached it. **Usable if the script wants moral complexity.**

### 5.7 Bockscar crew and Fr. George Zabelka
- **Bockscar crew:** pilot Maj. Charles W. Sweeney; co-pilot 1Lt. Charles Donald Albury; third pilot/observer 2Lt. Fred J. Olivi; navigator Capt. James Van Pelt; bombardier **Capt. Kermit K. Beahan**; weaponeer Cdr. Frederick Ashworth (Navy); radar countermeasures 1Lt. **Jacob Beser** (only man on *both* Hiroshima and Nagasaki missions); tail gunner Sgt. Albert Dehart; flight engineer M/Sgt John Kuharek; radio op Cpl. Abe Spitzer; radar Sgt. Edward Buckley; assistant engineer/scanner Sgt. Ray Gallagher.
- Olivi and Gallagher became the most publicly outspoken later in life. Beahan publicly expressed hope "**I am the last man to drop an atomic bomb in anger.**"
- **Fr. George B. Zabelka** (1915–1992) — Catholic military chaplain at the 509th Composite Group base on Tinian. **Heard confessions and gave blessings to both Enola Gay and Bockscar crews.** Over 20 years of reflection moved from just-war thomism to complete Gospel nonviolence. Publicly announced his conversion in a **1975 Christmas letter**. Received the **Pax Christi Pope Paul VI Teacher of Peace Award in 1985**. Walked the Bethlehem Peace Pilgrimage from Bangor, WA to Bethlehem. Died 1992.

### 5.8 Satin-tier surprises (act IV)
- **Nagasaki was never the intended target.** Kokura was #1 day-of. Even the downtown Nagasaki aim point was missed — Fat Man drifted ~3 km northwest to detonate over Urakami, the Christian quarter. **The most Christian square kilometer in Asia was destroyed by accident of cumulus cover and cross-wind.**
- **The cathedral was built by the descendants of the Christians who had been tortured on that exact ground.** Urakami parishioners pooled money and bricks themselves over 1895–1925, on land where fumi-e had been stomped for two centuries.
- **The Angelus bell rang on Christmas Eve 1945** — four months after the bomb, from a hole in the rubble. Its twin was recovered in 2025, and both rang together for the 80th anniversary.
- **The man who blessed Bockscar became a peace activist.** Zabelka's 1975 letter of renunciation is one of the more extraordinary conversions in 20th-century Catholic ethics.

## 7. Character Anchor Bios (for script personification)

### 7.1 Dutch *tsūji* (Act II, Dejima)
- Composite (historically grounded — Motoki Yoshinaga / Yoshio Kōgyū). Lived 1741–1788.
- Four-generation hereditary line of interpreters. Born knowing he would never choose his profession.
- His grandfather lost five years' stipend to a single mistranslation. His uncle died in prison after the Siebold-adjacent Takahashi Kageyasu case.
- Speaks Dutch to Kaempfer's successors, writes Latin medical terms in katakana, survives on a diet of rice and imported Javanese sugar the Dutch paid him with.
- Script beat: **He translates the word "Christian" into Japanese for the shōgun's officials every year during the annual inventory.** That word — forbidden in his country for a century — passes through his mouth daily.

### 7.2 Kakure kirishitan peasant (Act III, Urakami)
- Composite woman modeled loosely on **Sugimoto Yuri** (Isabelina) — born ~1813, Urakami village.
- Her grandmother taught her orasho as pure sound, not meaning. She knows the Ave Maria as *"Ame Maria karassa binno domisu…"* — it's a rhythm, not a phrase.
- She performs fumi-e every January. She whispers *konchirisan* every January night.
- She has a Songzi Guanyin statue in the family altar, which is actually Mary. The lotus in Kannon's hand, in her family, is Christ.
- Script beat (1865): **She is among the fifteen. She is the one who asks where Mary's statue is.** She lives another 28 years and sees her community exiled, tortured, and returned. She dies in 1893, 80, never having met the priest of 1914 Urakami.

### 7.3 Urakami doctor (Act IV, 9 Aug 1945)
- Modeled directly on **Nagai Takashi** (1908–1951).
- Radiologist. Catholic convert through marriage. Already dying of chronic leukemia from X-ray exposure when the bomb falls.
- At 11:02 at a darkened X-ray desk, ~700 m from hypocenter. Feels the flash before the sound. Loses ~807 colleagues.
- Returns home days later. Finds his wife as ashes. Finds her rosary in her hand-bones.
- Writes *Nagasaki no Kane* from bed, dying. Dies 1951 at 43.
- Script beat: **He is the voice that narrates the closing 90 seconds.** Restraint, warmth, no shouting.

## 9. Flagged Uncertainties / Discrepancies

- **Exile totals:** 3,394 vs 3,414 across sources. Death toll 600 vs 662 vs 680. Present as "**~3,400 exiled, ~660 died**."
- **Sugimoto Yuri's name spelling** varies (Isabelina / Izaberina / Isabellina). Safe English rendering: **"Isabelina Yuri Sugimoto"**. Japanese Wiki Corpus and McClelland 2025 use "Izaberina Sugimoto Yuri."
- **"15 villagers"** number is standard; a few sources just say "a group." Go with 15.
- **End of fumi-e:** mostly **1858**; one source says 1856.
- **Urakami Catholic dead 1945:** **8,500** (Nagai, Japanese sources) to **9,600** (some Anglo-Catholic). Either cite Nagai with "8,500" or present the range.
- **Cathedral completion:** main 1914, bell towers 1925. "Largest in East Asia when completed" most accurately dates to **1925**.
- **Cathedral rebuild:** **on the same original site, 1959** — not a different location. (README previously said "different location" — to be corrected.)
- **Feast of the Transfiguration** is 6 August (Hiroshima), not Nagasaki. The stronger Nagasaki-specific hook is the **15 August Feast of the Assumption** — Urakami faithful were actively preparing for it in the cathedral when the bomb fell.
